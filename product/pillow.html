Elevation: Fighting Swelling with Gravity

The simplest way to reduce pain and swelling after surgery is also the most overlooked. Elevating your leg above your heart is a non-negotiable part of the "RICE" method.

The Art of the "Pillow Stack"

During my recovery, I actually didn't own a professional wedge pillow. I learned the "art of the stack", using three or four standard pillows to create a ramp that kept my knee stable and high. It worked, but it was a constant battle to keep them from sliding apart in the middle of the night.

My Honest Advice: Even though I made it work with what I had, I strongly recommend investing in a dedicated elevation pillow. A wedge provides a consistent, stable angle that standard pillows just can't match.

"Elevation isn't just about comfort; it's about drainage. Gravity is the only thing that will move that fluid out of your knee joint when you're resting."

The Travel Hack: Inflatable Pillows

The real challenge starts when you leave your sofa. On long flights, keeping a surgical knee down in a cramped seat is a recipe for a massive flare-up. I used inflatable travel pillows (the ones designed for footwells) to turn my economy seat into a makeshift recliner. It made a world of difference in managing swelling during international travel.

Elevation Pro-Tips

Above the Heart

For elevation to actually work against swelling, your knee must be higher than your heart. If you're just propping it up on a footstool while sitting upright, you aren't getting the full benefit of gravity.

Avoid the "Knee Fold"

Never put a small pillow directly under the knee joint. This keeps the knee bent and can lead to stiffness. Support the entire leg from the calf down to the heel to keep the joint neutral.

Deflate for Portability

If you're traveling, an inflatable wedge is your best friend. It takes up no space in your bag but provides instant relief in hotel rooms or airplanes where extra pillows are scarce.

Heel Freedom

If you use the "stack" method, ensure your heel is slightly hanging off or very softly padded. Constant pressure on the heel while elevated can become surprisingly painful after a few hours.
